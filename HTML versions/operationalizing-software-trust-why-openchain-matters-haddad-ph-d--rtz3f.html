Strengthening Trust, Transparency, and Compliance in the Software Supply Chain

Over the past decade, the software supply chain has moved from a technical implementation concern to a strategic enterprise risk. Software has become central to every product and service, raising the responsibility bar for organizations to ensure that the software they ship is secure, compliant, and transparently governed.

This is where the OpenChain Project, hosted by the Linux Foundation, enters the picture.

For those unfamiliar, OpenChain defines industry standards for managing open source license compliance and security assurance across complex software supply chains. It provides a shared language for companies to communicate expectations and verify open source due diligence internally and with partners.

Yet, many organizations are still sitting on the sidelines.

After helping build and advise dozens of OSPOs over the past 15 years, from startups to multinationals, I can say this with confidence:

If your organization consumes or distributes open source software (hint: you do), OpenChain is not optional. It’s inevitable.

From Internal Chaos to Supply Chain Trust

In its early days, OpenChain tackled a fundamental problem: companies were approaching open source license compliance in completely inconsistent ways. Legal teams struggled with ad hoc spreadsheets (I've been there in the early 2000s). Engineering had no aligned process. Suppliers submitted opaque black boxes. Downstream consumers had no way to verify due diligence.

What OpenChain introduced was something deceptively simple and powerful:

A standardized way to define, measure, and verify open source compliance programs.

Its original specification focused on license compliance process requirements, essentially, “What does a competent open source compliance program look like?”

Over time, OpenChain evolved. It expanded to cover security assurance (with the OpenChain Security Assurance Spec), gained ISO standardization (ISO/IEC 5230), and became a reference point in government regulations.

Why It Matters Now: Regulation, Risk, and Readiness

We are entering a new regulatory reality:

  • In the US, Executive Order 14028 on Cybersecurity mandates software vendors to provide Software Bills of Materials (SBOMs) and verify the provenance of their code.

  • In the EU, the Cyber Resilience Act and Product Liability Directive explicitly hold manufacturers accountable for the digital components in their products, including software licensing and security posture.

  • Globally, countries from Japan to China are building national open source strategies emphasizing governance, trust, and transparency.

The message is clear:

Your software supply chain is now part of your legal and operational liability.

OpenChain provides a clear, recognized pathway to demonstrate that your organization takes this seriously. It doesn’t replace SBOM generation or vulnerability scanning. It complements them by addressing the underlying processes that produce trustworthy results.

What OpenChain Offers OSPOs and Legal Teams

For OSPOs, OpenChain is a framework that helps scale governance across teams, business units, and suppliers. It removes guesswork from onboarding, tooling decisions, and compliance verification. It turns reactive work into a proactive process design.

For Legal and Compliance, it offers risk reduction through standardization and transparency. OpenChain helps legal teams establish audit-ready processes, streamline supplier engagement, and reduce ambiguity around obligations and responsibilities.

In my experience, organizations that adopt OpenChain see:

  • Fewer legal escalations related to open source software compliance

  • Faster procurement and onboarding of third-party software

  • Improved trust in security and compliance assertions

  • Greater internal alignment between engineering, product, and legal

Where to Start

You don’t need to “boil the ocean.” OpenChain provides resources to begin at your own pace:

  • Read the Spec: The OpenChain ISO/IEC 5230 standard is public and free.

  • Adopt Self-Certification: You can align with the standard without hiring an auditor.

  • Join the Community: Engage with OpenChain Working Groups and learn from peers across many industries.

  • Map Your OSPO Maturity: OpenChain can serve as a north star for OSPO development, aligned with other frameworks such as SPDX, OpenSSF best practices, and the OSPO lifecycle.

The Future: A Common Language for Software Assurance

Looking ahead, I believe OpenChain will become a core element of how organizations signal trust across the software supply chain for license compliance, security assurance, supplier governance, and digital product certification.

OSPOs will increasingly be tasked with managing inbound risk, outbound assurance, and ecosystem accountability. Legal teams will look for standardized processes to keep up with regulatory mandates. OpenChain, combined with SBOM tooling, software composition analysis tools, and OSPO frameworks, is the connective tissue.

Final Thoughts

We’ve reached a tipping point. As governments legislate, customers demand transparency, and ecosystems scale, the organizations that invest in standardized open source governance will lead. OpenChain is a proven, community-driven way to get there.

If you’re thinking about how your OSPO can scale trust, reduce audit friction, and stay ahead of regulation, now is the time to engage with OpenChain. Software supply chain assurance is not optional.
