Open Source AI - What's 2020 Looking Like?

Created on 2019-12-21 20:46

Published on 2019-12-22 03:10

As 2019 draws to a close, there are many daily posts from various industry luminaries publishing 2020 predictions relevant to their domains, verticals and industries. This piece is no different with the exception that I am not a luminary nor claim to be one. This short article is my own attempt to express and try to make sense of everything happening around me in open source and specifically the AI domain.

There are three questions which I will address in this post on this gray, rainy Saturday in Seattle:

  1. What's so special about open source that applies to AI?
  2. What are the challenges we see in this seemingly thriving ecosystem?
  3. What are our expectations for 2020?

The Attraction of Open Source to AI

Open source has been eating the software world across multiple industries and has already become the de facto method of developing software, with countless industries - from telecom to entertainment, automotive to energy - increasingly relying upon it. Today, more leading-edge software development occurs inside open source communities than ever before, and it is becoming increasingly difficult for proprietary projects to keep up with the rapid pace of development that open source offers. Proprietary development has been almost exclusive in creating value or adding value to billions of dollars worth of existing open source software. You've probably heard of the Pareto principle or the 80/20 rule which applies to advanced industries and technology verticals today relying on open source software - 80% of deployed code is open source and 20% is proprietary software is where most of the customer facing value creation happens (i.e. differentiation).

Open source is a market accelerant and the advantages it offers across various industry verticals apply to AI, as illustrated in the figure below.

Figure 1: Advantages provided by open source

AI is no different that other technology sectors, and is increasingly becoming dominated by open source software that brings together multiple collaborators and organizations. Everyone will benefit from the increased innovation that comes from jointly collaborating on machine and deep learning technologies and projects. However, none of the advantages apply specifically to AI more than other domains.

So where's the value with open source for AI?

The true advantages of open source with respect to AI (as a very broad domain) lies in five core areas as show in Figure 2. The essence of these five areas is the openness, transparency, and accountability characteristics of the open source development model itself.

Collaborating as a community (companies, not-for-profit organizations, NGOs, individuals, etc.) to design and develop:

  1. Methods to detect and mitigate bias in datasets and models
  2. Methods to detect alterations or tampering in datasets and models
  3. Methods to enhance understandability of models
  4. Methods to ensure true provenance of data sets and models
  5. Methods to clean, sort, anonymize, tag, and publish data sets

Figure 2: Core advantages provided by open source to AI

All of these points above can be summarized by Trusted and Responsible AI. There are over a dozen initiatives that aim to produce some sort of outcome in relation to Trusted AI or Responsible AI. Companies are launching internal efforts to produce AI guidelines and principles aligned with the concepts of establishing trust and responsibility in developing AI infused products and services.

Going into 2020, we expect growing emphasis on trusted and responsible AI, moving away from just guidelines and principles into toolkits that can perform audits and report adherence with said guidelines and principles. This requires a collective effort and the transparency provided by the open source development methodology.

Challenges in Open Source AI

There are several inter-winded challenges that we can observe in the open source AI ecosystem. We summarize them in Figure 3.

Figure 3: Challenges pyramid

Starting from the bottom of the pyramid and going up:

  1. Fragmentation, lack of integration efforts across projects, and lack of cross pollination.
  2. Lack of a fair and neutral governance that provides equal footing to all project participants/contributors.
  3. Many of the projects were initially proprietary efforts to fill a need for a specific product or service, and the open source spin-off came about as an effort to share development costs with others trying to solve the same challenge. After all the value is in the data, models and apps and not necessarily in the enabling libraries and frameworks.
  4. Who owns and manages the projects assets? This is an easy answer for a small project with few participants. However, as the project grows and multiple companies become involved, things start to get complex and the need arises for a neutral body to take over the ownership and management of these assets, making them available to all project participants.
  5. With all of the above points, there is a glass ceiling of investment and adoption. A project can provide great and much needed functionality, but still lack adoption in the absence of an open governance and a neutral body managing its assets.

Side story

In February 2013, I joined Samsung Electronics to establish their open source global organization. During that time, my colleague and friend Guy Martin joined us as Director of Open Source Strategy. We were in constant battle with the culture at that time and the belief that leadership in open source project is be achieved by control. It was bad enough that Guy had a slide in internals talks stating:

Leadership != Control

With that in mind, and looking at the current landscape of open source, I will borrow the same concept to state:

Open Governance != Open Source

A project can have an OSI-approved open source license, be published on GitHub, and still remain under the primary influence and control of its founding company - because Open Governance != Open Source. Establishing a fair and open governance model makes a big impact on the project in attracting new user base and new contributors - who at some point want to become committers, maintainers and have influence on the project, its development roadmap, and any critical decisions that need to be made. For many companies, this is a critical concern: who wants to invest resources and commit to adopting it in a product when they have no clear path to leadership within the project and no clear path on how to be part of the decision making process?

More Open Sourcing Activities

Many more companies will be open sourcing their internal AI related projects in order to build communities around those projects and benefits from the open source advantages. Collaboration at the platform level (frameworks, libraries, platform) will enable companies and communities to harmonize across a set of critical projects, put together, will create a comprehensive open source stack in the AI, ML and DL space. Companies who were the first to open source their AI efforts (2014 and 2015) have already captured early mover advantages, and we expect this trend to only accelerate in 2020.

More Projects Hosted Under Foundations

Companies have also realized that opening their code under an open source license is not enough (open governance != open source). Structured and neutral governance is required so that other organizations are comfortable and encouraged to join as well as contribute to projects, becoming an active member of the community.

One way to achieve that is to host the project under a foundation such as The Linux Foundation. I can't come across as completely objective, as I work for the Linux Foundation, but with close to 200 hosted projects, a PMO office, an professional events team, and more than 1,400 member companies backing tens of thousands of active developers, the value proposition of such a Foundation is incredibly high.

Hosted projects could benefit from the experience of the Foundation in multiple ways:

  • Neutral facilitator and a trust enabler: Trust that decision makers are unbiased, that the project will stick, is worth investing R&D budget, and that the intentions of other participants are true. Neutrality and trust allows projects to thrive. These two factors are required for participants to be able to make firm strategic commitments, enable participants to form development alliances, and encourage participants to contribute valuable features that make the project better.
  • Knowledge, expertise and credibility: Well-launched projects will benefit from a startup momentum for a long time. However, this requires help from people who have done it successfully before, knowledge of what works and what doesn’t, and existing credibility in launching and managing large scale multi-tenants open source projects.
  • In addition, projects would benefit from the Foundation's services tailored to the project such as IT and infrastructure, marketing and communications, legal support, events, and so on.

LF AI is an umbrella project under the Linux Foundation whose goal is to build and support an open AI community, and drive open source innovation in the AI, ML and DL domains by enabling collaboration and the creation of new opportunities for all the members of the community.

Data, the new oil?

Regardless of whether you believe that data is the new oil or not, you would probably agree that data is an important asset to have. You can't possibly have all the data you need or want, and there is a need to start sharing data and collaborating on building such data sets.

There are several specific challenges in relation to open data:

  • Open sourcing decision - For example: What are the internal reviews that a company should undergo when it shares data with an open source project?
  • Licensing - The Linux Foundation pioneered this effort with the creation of the Community Data License Agreement to enable sharing of data similar to what open source licenses did to the sharing of source code
  • Governance - For example: What type of governance should we have in place for shared data sets?
  • Policies - For example: What policies are necessary to address personal data?
  • Privacy - For example: What are the procedures on how to remediate inclusion of personal data in a repository intended only for non-personal data?
  • Data sign-off - For example: Do the DCO sign-off procedures we use with code work with contributions of data?
  • Infrastructure - For example: What kind of infrastructure can be used for hosting shared data?
  • Provenance - For example: How can data provenance best be tracked?

Going into 2020, we expect more focused efforts in all of these areas. There will be increased discussion on critical questions related to data security and privacy, in addition to policies and tools that will need to be developed to safeguard privacy, anonymize data and remove bias. 

Open Source to Spur Innovation

As many AI technical experts and luminaries are voicing concerns over slowdown in innovation in deep learning and machine learning and warning of a possible new winter, the open source methodology becomes even more appealing as a model of cooperation to advance innovation around core, shared enabling technologies. You can't possibly hire all the smart people in the world working on these challenges. Open source allows a model to collaborate with all such people across the globe.

Consolidation

We expect some level of consolidation to happen around frameworks and libraries due to the high level of fragmentation that currently exists. The outcome of this consolidation is likely to be win-win. Successful projects will grab their share of additional contributors, and contributors from business as well as academia from older projects will have gained the knowledge, experience and skills necessary to meet the demand for talent in this space on new projects.
